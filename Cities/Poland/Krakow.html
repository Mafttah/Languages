Welcome to Krakow

Krakow is Poland's former capital and cultural heart, miraculously spared from WWII destruction preserving its medieval and Renaissance architecture. The stunning Main Market Square, Wawel Royal Castle, and Jewish Kazimierz district create extraordinary historic ensemble. Once home to Polish kings and now a vibrant university city, Krakow offers Gothic churches, underground medieval market, proximity to Auschwitz, and the atmospheric salt mines. The city embodies Polish culture, history, and artistic traditions in remarkably intact medieval urban fabric.

Places to Visit

St. Mary's Basilica

This Gothic church dominates the Main Market Square with asymmetrical towers. The famous wooden altarpiece by Veit Stoss is one of Europe's largest Gothic altars. Every hour, a trumpeter plays the Hejna≈Ç from the taller tower, stopping mid-melody commemorating a 13th-century Mongol attack. The interior features stunning vaulted ceiling and stained glass. The basilica represents Krakow's medieval wealth and continuing traditions through the hourly trumpet call.

Europe's largest medieval town square features the Cloth Hall, St. Mary's Basilica, and surrounding historic townhouses. The vast 200x200 meter space hosts markets, street performers, and horse-drawn carriages. Underground museum reveals medieval market remains. The square served as Krakow's commercial and social center for 800 years. Its scale, architectural harmony, and vibrant atmosphere make it Poland's finest public space and Krakow's beating heart.

Wawel Cathedral

This Gothic cathedral atop Wawel Hill served as Polish coronation and burial site for centuries. The cathedral features chapel additions from different eras including the Renaissance Sigismund Chapel. Royal tombs, the Sigismund Bell, and treasury hold national treasures. The cathedral represents Polish statehood, religious tradition, and architectural evolution. As the spiritual heart of Poland where kings were crowned and buried, it embodies national identity and continuity.

Planty Park

This green ring encircling the Old Town follows former medieval walls' path creating 4-kilometer tree-lined promenade. The park features monuments, fountains, gardens, and provides pleasant walking route around the historic center. Created in the 19th century after walls' demolition, Planty represents urban transformation while preserving defensive perimeter as green belt. The park connects major gates and offers peaceful escape while maintaining proximity to monuments.

Dragon's Den

This cave beneath Wawel Hill features the legendary dragon's lair with a fire-breathing dragon statue at its entrance. According to legend, a dragon terrorized Krakow until defeated. The cave tour reveals natural formations and connects to legend. The dragon statue attracts children and tourists. The Den represents Krakow's mythological foundations and adds whimsical element to historic Wawel Hill while entertaining visitors with Polish folklore and natural wonder.

Ulica Kanonicza

This medieval street is Krakow's most beautiful with Renaissance and baroque townhouses where clergy once lived. Pope John Paul II resided at No. 19 as a young priest. The street's architectural harmony and historic character create atmospheric walk from Wawel to Main Square. The cobblestones, historic facades, and papal connection make it essential for experiencing Krakow's preserved medieval urban fabric and religious heritage.
