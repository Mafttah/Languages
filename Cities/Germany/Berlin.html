About Berlin

Berlin is the capital and largest city of Germany, by both area and population. With 3.7 million inhabitants, it has the highest population within its city limits of any city in the European Union, and the fifth largest in Europe after Istanbul, Moscow, London and St Petersburg.

Brandenburg Gate (Brandenburger Tor)

The Brandenburg Gate, which has become the symbol of Berlin, represents Germany's unified history and Europe's hope for peace. Commissioned by Prussian King Friedrich Wilhelm II in the late 18th century, the structure stands out as a magnificent 12-column gateway inspired by ancient Greek architecture. One of the most symbolic points separating the city's west and east sides, this gate was also known as the border crossing between two worlds during the Berlin Wall era.

Berlin Wall

The Berlin Wall is a structure that has left a deep mark not only on Germany's memory but on the entire world's. Built in 1961, it not only divided West and East Germany but also became a symbol of the Cold War. When this concrete barrier, which had separated families, lives, and freedoms for decades, fell in 1989, the fate of both Germany and Europe changed.

Berlin Cathedral (Berliner Dom)

The Berliner Dom, with its magnificent dome standing out on the Berlin skyline, is one of the city's most striking structures, both for its architectural grandeur and its religious significance. Completed in 1905, this Protestant cathedral is one of Germany's largest churches.

Alexanderplatz

Alexanderplatz, one of Berlin's most central and vibrant squares, is a large public space where many of the city's transportation routes intersect, surrounded by shopping malls, hotels, and iconic landmarks. It offers an ideal starting point, especially for first-time travelers to Berlin. The famous World Clock (Weltzeituhr), located in the center of the square, is a popular tourist destination, drawing crowds to gather around it every hour.

Checkpoint Charlie

Checkpoint Charlie, one of the most symbolic points of the Cold War era, was the most famous of the border crossings that separated the American and Soviet-controlled areas in the past.

Tiergarten & Victory Monument

Located in the heart of Berlin, Tiergarten is one of the city's largest and most beloved parks. This vast 200-hectare green space is a natural escape for residents and tourists alike, whether for a breather, a walk, or to explore by bike. The Victory Column (Siegess√§ule), located near the park's center, commemorates Germany's military victories in the 19th century. Topped by a golden statue of the goddess of victory (Viktoria), this column stands 67 meters tall and offers visitors a sweeping view of Berlin.

Gendarmenmarkt

Gendarmenmarkt is a square in Berlin containing historic buildings such as the Konzerthaus, the French Cathedral, and the German Cathedral. The square was originally created in the late 1600s as Linden-Markt. It was rebuilt in 1773 by Georg Christian Unger.

Potsdamer Platz

After German reunification, the square quickly transformed into a completely different place. Today, it's filled with skyscrapers, shopping and entertainment centers, upscale restaurants, and businesses. Street performers and remnants of the Berlin Wall are also among the most important elements that add to the square's popularity.

Kreuzberg

Kreuzberg, one of Berlin's most vibrant, talked-about, and also controversial districts, best reflects the city's multicultural landscape. With its immigrant population, alternative lifestyles, street art, and third-wave coffee shops, it's become a popular destination for both free-spirited travelers and the city's young generation.

Unter den Linden

Unter den Linden, one of Berlin's historic boulevards, takes its name from the magnificent linden trees lining both sides. This wide avenue, stretching from the Brandenburg Gate to the Berlin Cathedral, stands out as one of the city's oldest and most prestigious pedestrian walkways. Architectural structures dating from the 18th and 19th centuries, elegant mansions, university buildings, and cultural institutions line this route. Humboldt University, the Berlin State Library, and the German Historical Museum, in particular, reflect this avenue's academic and cultural richness.

Mauerpark

This expansive park, named after the Berlin Wall that once stood here, is not only a green space bearing traces of the past but also a hub of the city's cultural life. Its expansive grassy areas are filled with people picnicking, playing Frisbee, and simply sunbathing and reading. It's a wonderful spot, especially in fine weather, for those looking to observe Berlin's social fabric and experience the city's creative energy.

Kurf√ºrstendamm, one of the city's largest shopping streets, stretches for 3.5 kilometers in the center of Berlin, lined with modern buildings and historical structures. It is very lively at all hours of the day with high-quality stores where you can find products from famous brands, shopping malls, independent boutiques, roadside cafes and restaurants serving world cuisine.

Tempelhofer Feld

Tempelhofer Feld, one of Berlin's most extraordinary open-air spaces, once used as an airport, has now been transformed into a massive city park. Opened in the 1920s, Tempelhof Airport served as a key strategic hub during World War II and the Cold War. It's one of the few places where you can experience nature, freedom, and social life all at once.

Oberbaum Bridge

Situated over the River Spree, which flows through Berlin and offers stunning views, this historic 150-meter-long bridge is one of Berlin's most recognizable landmarks. Designed as an archway rising 28 meters above the river, the Oberbaum Bridge, which once formed the border between East and West Berlin along with the Berlin Wall, is a picturesque spot to take photos of, especially when illuminated at night. Or, you can simply take a leisurely stroll across the historic bridge and admire the breathtaking views of the River Spree.

Friedrichstra√üe

One of Berlin's most popular cultural and shopping streets, Frederick Street stretches from the historic Mitte district to the Kreuzberg district. Almost completely destroyed in World War II and only partially rebuilt after Berlin was divided into two, Frederick Street is one of Berlin's liveliest districts, boasting dozens of shops and five-star hotels.

Karl Marx Allee

Built by East Germany between 1952 and 1960 in the historic Mitte district, Karl Marx Boulevard, lined with Socialist monuments and buildings on every corner, will give you the opportunity to see a different, almost forgotten side of Berlin.

Bebelplatz

Located in the historic Mitte district, the impressive centuries-old buildings surrounding it offer a truly spectacular sight. Located right next to the Unter den Linden Boulevard, Bebel Square boasts a centuries-long history. In addition to its monumental statues, you can also see many impressive structures up close, including the Berlin Opera House, the Humboldt University Buildings, and St. Hedwig's Cathedral, the first Catholic church built in Prussia.
