Welcome to Valencia

Valencia is Spain's third-largest city, a vibrant Mediterranean metropolis that perfectly balances historic charm with futuristic architecture. Birthplace of paella and home to the spectacular City of Arts and Sciences, Valencia offers stunning beaches, the innovative Turia Gardens park, and a beautifully preserved old town. Famous for its March Fallas festival with giant burning sculptures, Valencia embodies Spanish creativity, culinary excellence, and Mediterranean lifestyle.

Catedral de Santa Mar√≠a de Valencia

Valencia's Gothic cathedral, built between the 13th and 15th centuries on a former mosque site, houses what's claimed to be the Holy Grail. The cathedral blends Gothic, Romanesque, Renaissance, and Baroque styles. Climb the 207 steps of the Miguelete bell tower for panoramic city views. The cathedral museum displays artworks by Goya and other masters. The building represents Valencia's layered history and religious significance.

2

Jardins del Turia

This 9-kilometer urban park follows the former course of the Turia River through Valencia's heart. After devastating floods in 1957, the river was diverted and the riverbed transformed into Spain's largest urban park. The gardens feature sports facilities, playgrounds, fountains, and connect major attractions including the City of Arts and Sciences. It's perfect for walking, cycling, or jogging while experiencing Valencia's commitment to green urban spaces.

3

Valencia Old Town

The historic center preserves Valencia's medieval layout with narrow streets, historic buildings, and charming plazas. The Barrio del Carmen neighborhood features vibrant street art alongside Gothic architecture. The old town includes the Silk Exchange, Central Market, cathedral, and countless tapas bars. Getting lost in these atmospheric streets reveals Valencia's authentic character, from traditional shops to trendy boutiques, creating a perfect blend of old and new.

4

Plaza de la Virgen

This beautiful square at the heart of the old town features the Turia Fountain representing Valencia's irrigation canals, the cathedral's Apostles' Door where the Water Tribunal meets weekly, and the Basilica of Our Lady of the Forsaken. The plaza buzzes with street musicians and outdoor cafes. Every Thursday at noon, the centuries-old Water Tribunal publicly resolves irrigation disputes in an oral tradition dating to Moorish times.

6

Plaza de la Reina

This elegant square adjacent to the cathedral features flower stalls, outdoor terraces, and the distinctive orange and palm trees typical of Valencia. The plaza serves as a popular meeting point and gateway to the old town. The cathedral's Miguelete tower dominates the square's skyline. Surrounded by ice cream shops and cafes, it's perfect for enjoying horchata, Valencia's traditional tiger nut drink, while people-watching.

7

Plaza del Ayuntamiento

Valencia's grandest square features the impressive City Hall with its clock tower, central fountain, and flower displays that change with seasons. This ceremonial plaza hosts major events including the Fallas festival's masclet√† daily firework displays. The square's monumental buildings showcase early 20th-century architecture. The plaza connects the old town with the modern city and serves as Valencia's civic heart where celebrations and protests alike take place.

8

Plaza Redonda

This unique circular plaza hidden in the old town features traditional craft shops, haberdasheries, and ceramics stores arranged around a central fountain. Built in the 19th century, the round shape makes it instantly recognizable and charming. The plaza specializes in traditional Valencian products, embroidery, and souvenirs. On Sundays, a flea market fills the space. It's a delightful discovery offering authentic local shopping away from tourist traps.

9

Plaza de Lope de Vega

This small, charming square in the Carmen neighborhood is surrounded by colorful buildings and lively bars. The plaza features outdoor terraces perfect for evening drinks and captures the bohemian spirit of the Carmen district. Less touristy than central squares, it's popular with locals and students. The intimate atmosphere and surrounding street art make it feel like a neighborhood living room where Valencians gather to socialize.

10

Gulliver Park

This unique playground in the Turia Gardens features a giant 70-meter sculpture of Gulliver lying down, with slides, ramps, and stairs covering his body. Children can climb over the giant figure inspired by Swift's novel. The creative design makes it one of Spain's most unusual playgrounds. While primarily for children, the imaginative concept appeals to all ages and represents Valencia's innovative approach to public spaces.

The garden represents Valencia's aristocratic past and offers a serene retreat from urban bustle.
