Welcome to Venice

Venice is an extraordinary city built on 118 islands connected by 400 bridges across the Venetian Lagoon. This UNESCO World Heritage Site enchants with its intricate canal network, gondolas gliding through waterways, and Byzantine-Gothic architecture. Once a powerful maritime republic controlling Mediterranean trade, Venice preserves palaces, churches, and artistic treasures testifying to past glory. The unique car-free environment, romantic ambiance, and unparalleled beauty make Venice one of the world's most magical destinations.

Places to Visit

Ponte di Rialto

Venice's most famous bridge spans the Grand Canal with a single stone arch completed in 1591. The covered bridge features shops lining both sides selling jewelry, souvenirs, and leather goods. For centuries the only way to cross the Grand Canal on foot, the Rialto represents Venice's engineering prowess and commercial spirit. The views from the bridge over gondolas and palazzi capture quintessential Venice. It remains an iconic symbol and busy crossing point.

Piazza San Marco

Venice's principal square is the only piazza in the city - others being called campi. Surrounded by stunning architecture including St. Mark's Basilica, Doge's Palace, and Procuratie buildings, the square hosts the famous Caff√® Florian and Caff√® Quadri. Pigeons, orchestras, acqua alta flooding, and carnival celebrations make it Venice's ceremonial center. Napoleon called it "the finest drawing room in Europe." The square represents Venetian power and artistic achievement.

Basilica di San Marco

This Byzantine masterpiece features golden mosaics covering over 8,000 square meters depicting biblical scenes. Built in the 11th century to house St. Mark's relics, the basilica showcases five domes, marble floors, and treasures looted during the Fourth Crusade including the bronze horses. The opulent decoration reflects Venice's wealth and Eastern connections. The basilica represents Venetian power, Byzantine artistic influence, and religious devotion in overwhelming visual splendor.

Strada Nova

This wide street connecting the train station to Rialto is one of Venice's busiest commercial thoroughfares. Created in the 19th century by filling a canal, Strada Nova features shops, restaurants, and constant pedestrian flow. The street provides direct route through Cannaregio district, offering glimpses of authentic Venetian life. Though touristy, it showcases how Venice adapted its canal network while maintaining character through surrounding neighborhoods.

Basilica di Santi Giovanni e Paolo

This vast Gothic church, Venice's largest, served as the doges' funeral church. Built by Dominicans in the 14th-15th centuries, it contains tombs of 25 doges and numerous artistic treasures including works by Bellini and Veronese. The imposing brick facade and spacious interior demonstrate Dominican Gothic architecture. The church represents Venetian power and piety while showcasing the republic's greatest families' memorial traditions in magnificent artistic setting.

Campo Santa Margherita

This lively square in Dorsoduro represents authentic Venetian neighborhood life. The large campo hosts a morning market, surrounded by cafes, bars, and the former church of Santa Margherita. Students from nearby university, locals shopping, and children playing create vibrant atmosphere. Evening aperitivo culture thrives here away from San Marco crowds. The square exemplifies living Venice where residents maintain community traditions and social life in an authentic setting.
