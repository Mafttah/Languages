Welcome to Bologna

Bologna is known by three nicknames: La Rossa (the red) for its terracotta buildings, La Dotta (the learned) for its ancient university founded in 1088, and La Grassa (the fat) for exceptional cuisine including rag√π, tortellini, and mortadella. This vibrant Emilian city features medieval towers, 40km of porticoes creating covered walkways, and authentic atmosphere balancing student energy with culinary tradition. Bologna preserves genuine Italian character while offering cultural depth, gastronomic excellence, and architectural beauty.

Places to Visit

Piazza Maggiore

Bologna's main square serves as the heart of civic life surrounded by medieval palaces including Palazzo Comunale, Palazzo del Podest√†, and the unfinished facade of San Petronio basilica. The vast irregular space hosts events, markets, and serves as gathering point. The square represents Bologna's medieval commune government and continues as the social center where Bolognesi meet, protest, and celebrate in a magnificent ensemble of civic architecture.

Basilica di San Petronio

This massive Gothic basilica dominates Piazza Maggiore with an unfinished facade - planned marble cladding never completed. The interior features stunning chapels, the world's longest sundial meridian line, and capacity for 28,000 people. San Petronio represents Bologna's ambition to outdo St. Peter's in Rome, ultimately thwarted by the Pope. The basilica showcases Bologna's independence and civic pride in a sacred space reflecting the city's complex relationship with papal authority.

Piazza del Nettuno

Adjacent to Piazza Maggiore, this square features Giambologna's magnificent Neptune Fountain from 1566. The bronze Neptune and fountain nymphs create baroque masterpiece. The square connects to the main piazza while maintaining distinct character. The Neptune statue represents Bologna's artistic patronage and fountain engineering. The theatrical sculpture and its location make it Bologna's most photographed monument after the Two Towers, embodying Renaissance artistic achievement.

Basilica di Santo Stefano

This complex of seven interconnected churches (now four remain) is nicknamed "the Seven Churches" and represents Bologna's most atmospheric religious site. The Romanesque buildings include octagonal baptistery, courtyard with Pilate's basin, and ancient crypts. The complex's mysterious atmosphere and layered history spanning early Christianity to medieval times create unique spiritual setting. Santo Stefano represents Bologna's religious heritage in intimate, contemplative spaces contrasting with grand basilicas.

Via dell'Indipendenza

Bologna's main street connects the train station to Piazza Maggiore, lined with porticoes, shops, and cafes. The 19th-century boulevard provides direct route through the city center under covered walkways. The street represents Bologna's modern commercial development while maintaining the portico tradition. The constant pedestrian flow, retail options, and architectural harmony make it central artery experiencing Bologna's blend of accessibility and architectural heritage.

Piazza Santo Stefano

This atmospheric triangular square fronting the Santo Stefano basilica complex features elegant palaces and cafes with outdoor seating. The medieval and Renaissance buildings create harmonious ensemble. The square's intimate scale and proximity to the basilica make it perfect for enjoying Bologna's refined atmosphere. The combination of religious architecture, aristocratic palaces, and contemporary cafe culture represents Bologna's layered urban character in one of its most beautiful spaces.

Giardini Margherita

Bologna's largest park offers 26 hectares of green space with a lake, walking paths, playgrounds, and outdoor cafes. Created in 1879, the English-style park provides escape from the dense medieval center. Students, families, and joggers enjoy the park's amenities including summer concerts and film screenings. The park represents Bologna's commitment to public green space and serves as the city's outdoor living room during warm months.

Basilica di San Francesco

This Gothic-Romanesque church features distinctive flying buttresses rare in Italian architecture and French Gothic influences. The simple brick facade and soaring interior create austere beauty. The adjacent tombs of medieval glossatori (law scholars) reflect Bologna's university traditions. The basilica represents Bologna's connection to Gothic northern Europe while showcasing brick architecture typical of Emilian building traditions in a major Franciscan church.

Piazza Galvani

This square honors scientist Luigi Galvani with his statue and hosts the Archiginnasio, Bologna University's historic headquarters. The square connects commercial streets with the university district. The neoclassical architecture and academic associations represent Bologna's intellectual traditions. The square serves as gateway to the university area while maintaining connection to the historic center, embodying Bologna's identity as La Dotta, the learned city.

Piazza Giuseppe Verdi

This lively square in the university district features the Teatro Comunale opera house and serves as student gathering point. The nightlife around the piazza attracts young crowds to surrounding bars and clubs. The square represents Bologna's contemporary vitality and student culture. The mix of high culture through the theater and popular student energy creates dynamic atmosphere showcasing Bologna as a living university city with youthful character.

Monte della Guardia

This hill south of Bologna is crowned by the Sanctuary of Madonna di San Luca reached by the world's longest portico with 666 arches covering 3.8km. The climb or bus ride rewards with panoramic Bologna views and the hilltop sanctuary. Pilgrims have walked this covered path since the 18th century. The portico represents Bologna's architectural tradition extended to monumental scale while the sanctuary offers spiritual retreat and spectacular vistas.

Fontana del Nettuno

Giambologna's Neptune Fountain in Piazza del Nettuno from 1566 features the powerful bronze Neptune with water nymphs. The fountain represents Renaissance sculpture mastery and hydraulic engineering. The muscular Neptune symbolizes papal power over waters. The fountain's central location and artistic quality make it Bologna's symbol beyond the Two Towers, showcasing the city's Renaissance artistic patronage and baroque theatrical expression in public space.

Santuario della Madonna di San Luca

This hilltop sanctuary houses a Byzantine icon of the Virgin Mary attributed to St. Luke. The baroque church accessed by the world's longest portico attracts pilgrims and offers spectacular views. Each May, the icon processes to Bologna's cathedral in centuries-old tradition. The sanctuary represents Bolognese faith, architectural ambition through the portico, and connection between city and surrounding hills. The pilgrimage tradition maintains medieval religious practices.

Santa Maria della Vita

This baroque church features the dramatic Compianto sul Cristo Morto terracotta sculpture group by Niccol√≤ dell'Arca depicting mourning over Christ's body. The emotional intensity and realism make it a Renaissance masterpiece. The church represents Bologna's artistic heritage and baroque religious expression. The sculpture's powerful emotion and artistic quality justify visiting this lesser-known church, showcasing Bologna's rich artistic patrimony beyond famous monuments.

Parco di San Pellegrino

This park on Bologna's hills offers walking trails, picnic areas, and escape from urban density. The park features natural landscape, Monte Donato views, and recreational facilities. Popular with locals for outdoor activities, it represents Bologna's access to Apennine foothills. The park provides green retreat while showcasing the city's topographical variety and connection to surrounding countryside, offering fresh air and nature within reach of the center.
