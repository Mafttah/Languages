Welcome to Rome

Rome is Italy's capital and one of the world's greatest cities, where ancient ruins coexist with Renaissance masterpieces and baroque fountains. Founded in 753 BCE, Rome was the heart of the Roman Empire and later the Catholic Church, leaving unparalleled artistic and architectural legacy. From the Colosseum and Roman Forum to Vatican Museums and Trevi Fountain, Rome offers endless historical treasures, world-class cuisine, and the dolce vita lifestyle that makes it eternally captivating.

Places to Visit

Colosseum

The Colosseum is Rome's most iconic monument, the largest amphitheater ever built. Completed in 80 CE, it held 50,000 spectators for gladiatorial contests and public spectacles. The elliptical structure showcases Roman engineering with its complex system of underground passages. Though partially ruined, the Colosseum remains a powerful symbol of Imperial Rome's might and entertainment culture, offering insight into ancient Roman society and architectural achievement.

Fontana di Trevi

This baroque masterpiece is Rome's largest and most famous fountain. Completed in 1762, it features Neptune's chariot pulled by seahorses emerging from Palazzo Poli's facade. Tradition says tossing a coin over your shoulder ensures return to Rome. The fountain's dramatic sculptures, powerful water flow, and tucked-away piazza location create magical atmosphere, especially illuminated at night. It represents baroque Rome's theatrical grandeur and enduring romantic appeal.

The Pantheon

This 2,000-year-old temple is the best-preserved Roman building, featuring the world's largest unreinforced concrete dome. The oculus opening creates dramatic light effects. Originally a pagan temple, it became a church in 609 CE. The Pantheon's perfect proportions, innovative engineering, and continuous use make it architecturally and historically extraordinary. Raphael's tomb and other notable burials add to its significance as a monument spanning Roman, Christian, and Renaissance heritage.

Fontana dei Quattro Fiumi

Bernini's masterpiece in Piazza Navona features four river gods representing the continents known in 1651: Nile, Ganges, Danube, and R√≠o de la Plata. The fountain supports an Egyptian obelisk and showcases baroque drama with muscular figures, animals, and plants. The fountain represents papal power, geographical knowledge, and Bernini's genius. Its location in the vibrant piazza creates perfect setting for appreciating baroque Rome's theatrical public spaces.

Santa Maria Maggiore Basilica

One of Rome's four major basilicas combines early Christian origins with baroque additions. Founded in the 5th century, it preserves magnificent ancient mosaics depicting Old Testament scenes. The coffered ceiling uses first gold from the Americas. The basilica's hybrid architecture, precious mosaics, and role as major pilgrimage destination make it essential for understanding Rome's Christian heritage and artistic evolution through centuries.

Chiesa di San Luigi dei Francesi

This French national church houses three revolutionary Caravaggio paintings in the Contarelli Chapel: The Calling of St. Matthew, St. Matthew and the Angel, and The Martyrdom of St. Matthew. Caravaggio's dramatic use of light and realism transformed art history. The church represents the Counter-Reformation's artistic patronage and Caravaggio's genius. The paintings alone justify visiting this otherwise modest church near Piazza Navona.

Chiesa di Sant'Ignazio di Loyola

This baroque church features an extraordinary trompe-l'oeil ceiling by Andrea Pozzo depicting St. Ignatius's apotheosis. The painted architectural perspective creates illusory dome and expansive heavenly scenes. Standing on the marked floor spot reveals the intended viewing angle for maximum effect. The church represents baroque art's theatrical ambitions and Jesuit order's Counter-Reformation influence through overwhelming visual splendor and optical illusion.

Basilica di Santa Sabina

This 5th-century church on Aventine Hill is Rome's finest early Christian basilica, preserving original proportions and atmosphere. The wooden doors from 430 CE feature the first known Crucifixion representation. Twenty-four Corinthian columns create classical harmony. The basilica offers peaceful retreat from tourist crowds and exemplifies early Christian architecture adapted from Roman basilica form, representing Christianity's transformation of pagan structures into sacred Christian spaces.

Basilica of St. Chrysogonus

This ancient church in Trastevere dates to the 4th century with 12th-century rebuilding. The interior features a beautiful Cosmatesque floor, impressive columns, and medieval frescoes. The church represents Trastevere's medieval Christian heritage and Rome's layered history with excavations revealing earlier structures. Less touristy than major basilicas, it offers authentic atmosphere and artistic treasures showcasing Rome's continuous Christian presence through changing architectural styles.

San Giovanni in Laterano

Rome's cathedral and the Pope's official ecclesiastical seat predates St. Peter's Basilica. Founded by Constantine in 324, it's Christianity's oldest public church. The baroque facade, massive nave, and Holy Stairs (Scala Santa) attract pilgrims. As "mother of all churches," it represents Rome's role as Christianity's center and papal authority. The baptistery's octagonal form influenced Christian architecture worldwide, making it architecturally and symbolically crucial.

Sistine Chapel

Michelangelo's ceiling and Last Judgment frescoes make this chapel humanity's greatest artistic achievement. The ceiling depicts Genesis including the iconic Creation of Adam. The altar wall's Last Judgment shows dramatic final reckoning. Beyond Michelangelo, other Renaissance masters contributed frescoes. The chapel serves as the papal conclave venue. The overwhelming artistry and spiritual intensity create an unforgettable experience at Vatican Museums' climax.

Cappella di San Zenone

This 9th-century chapel in Santa Prassede basilica is Rome's finest Byzantine mosaic work, nicknamed "Garden of Paradise." The golden mosaics covering walls and vault depict Christ, saints, and angels in brilliant Byzantine style. The chapel represents papal Rome's connection to Byzantine Constantinople and preserves exceptional artistic quality. Though small, it contains some of Rome's most precious medieval art, showcasing Eastern Christian influence on Western art.

Piazza Venezia

This major square at the foot of Capitoline Hill features the massive Vittorio Emanuele II Monument (Vittoriano). The square marks central Rome's crossroads where major streets converge. Mussolini addressed crowds from Palazzo Venezia balcony. The Vittoriano's white marble nicknamed "wedding cake" dominates the skyline. The square represents united Italy's ambitions and provides panoramic city views from its terrace, though its architecture divides opinions.

Piazza Navona

Built on Domitian's ancient stadium, this elongated baroque square features Bernini's Fountain of Four Rivers, Borromini's Sant'Agnese church, and lively atmosphere with artists, cafes, and street performers. The piazza exemplifies baroque Rome's transformation of ancient spaces. Christmas markets and outdoor dining create constant animation. The three fountains, surrounding palaces, and pedestrian-only space make it Rome's most beloved gathering spot showcasing baroque theatrical urbanism.

Piazza del Popolo

This grand elliptical square marks the northern entrance to Rome's historic center. The ancient Egyptian obelisk centers the space flanked by twin baroque churches. The Porta del Popolo gate welcomed pilgrims traveling Via Flaminia from northern Europe. Santa Maria del Popolo church contains Caravaggio masterpieces. Climbing to Pincio terrace reveals spectacular views. The square represents papal Rome's ceremonial gateway and neoclassical urban planning elegance.

Via del Corso

Rome's main shopping street runs straight from Piazza Venezia to Piazza del Popolo, following the ancient Via Lata. Named for horse races (corso) held here during Carnival, the pedestrian street features international brands, historic palaces, and churches. The street connects major monuments while offering retail therapy. Its length showcases Rome's layers from ancient Roman road to Renaissance palaces to contemporary commercial center.

Via dei Condotti

Rome's most fashionable street connects Piazza di Spagna with Via del Corso, lined with luxury boutiques including Gucci, Prada, and Bulgari. Named after conduits bringing water to Agrippa's baths, the elegant street represents Rome's luxury shopping district. The 18th-century Antico Caff√® Greco, frequented by Byron and Goethe, adds historical charm. Window shopping here epitomizes Roman elegance and la dolce vita lifestyle amid designer flagship stores.

La Piccola Londra

This charming neighborhood near Piazza del Popolo features Art Nouveau villas, tree-lined streets, and early 20th-century architecture resembling London residential areas. The district's distinctive character offers surprising contrast to Rome's baroque and ancient architecture. The peaceful streets with their gardens and elegant buildings represent Rome's belle √©poque expansion and architectural eclecticism, providing pleasant walks away from tourist crowds through a unique Roman neighborhood.

Piazza del Campidoglio

Michelangelo designed this hilltop square on Capitoline Hill, Rome's civic and symbolic heart since ancient times. The harmonious Renaissance space features geometric pavement pattern and is flanked by Capitoline Museums. The cordonata staircase provides ceremonial ascent. The square balances ancient Roman site with Renaissance design, representing continuity between ancient and modern Rome. The political and cultural significance combined with Michelangelo's genius make it architecturally and symbolically profound.

Piazza San Pietro

Bernini's masterpiece embraces visitors with colonnaded arms before St. Peter's Basilica. The elliptical space holds 300,000 people with 284 columns and 88 pilasters topped by saints. The Egyptian obelisk centers the piazza with two fountains. Standing on marked pavement discs aligns colonnade columns creating optical illusion. The square represents baroque Rome's theatrical religious spaces and papal authority, functioning as gateway to Vatican and Christianity's most sacred sites.

Villa Borghese

Rome's most elegant park covers 80 hectares with museums, gardens, lakes, and the famous Pincio terrace. Cardinal Scipione Borghese created the estate in the 17th century. The Borghese Gallery houses exceptional art including Bernini sculptures and Caravaggio paintings. The park offers rowing, cycling, and peaceful walks through landscaped gardens. It represents aristocratic Roman villa culture and provides green escape combining nature, art, and stunning city views.

Pincio

This hillside terrace at Villa Borghese's edge offers Rome's most romantic panoramic view over Piazza del Popolo toward Vatican. The 19th-century promenade features busts of Italian patriots, fountains, and the water clock. Sunset views attract couples and photographers. The Pincio represents Rome's belle √©poque elegance and provides perspective on the city's layout, connecting Villa Borghese gardens with historic center in a designed landscape beloved by Romans.

Caffarella Park

This ancient park along Via Appia Antica preserves pastoral landscape within Rome. The archaeological park features ruins, Roman tombs, the Nymphaeum of Egeria, and grazing sheep. The Caffarella valley maintains agricultural use dating to ancient Rome. Walking these fields reveals Rome's rural character and connection to its ancient pastoral origins. The park represents Rome's successful preservation of green space and archaeological heritage within urban sprawl.

Janiculum Hill

Rome's second-highest hill offers spectacular panoramic views but isn't counted among the traditional seven hills. The Garibaldi Monument, fountain, and Passeggiata del Gianicolo promenade attract Romans and visitors. The daily noon cannon firing maintains tradition. The hill represents Italy's unification with monuments to Garibaldi and independence heroes. The views encompass all central Rome creating perfect photo opportunity while the peaceful atmosphere offers romantic escape.

Campo de' Fiori

This lively square hosts Rome's most atmospheric food market weekday mornings, transforming into nightlife hub evenings. The statue of philosopher Giordano Bruno burned here in 1600 creates somber counterpoint to the vibrant market. Surrounding medieval streets house artisan shops and restaurants. The square represents authentic Roman life where locals shop for fresh produce, making it perfect for experiencing genuine Roman market culture and nightlife.

Palatine Hill

The most central of Rome's seven hills hosted emperors' palaces and legendary Romulus's founding. Archaeological excavations reveal imperial residences, gardens, and stunning frescoes. The hill offers peaceful atmosphere and excellent Forum views. As Rome's mythical birthplace where the she-wolf nursed Romulus and Remus, and later the imperial residence district, the Palatine represents Rome's evolution from legend to superpower showcasing ruins spanning centuries.

Ponte Sant'Angelo

Hadrian built this bridge in 134 CE to access his mausoleum (now Castel Sant'Angelo). Bernini's ten angel statues holding Passion instruments line the pedestrian bridge. The statues represent baroque artistry at its finest. The bridge provides classic views of St. Peter's dome and Castel Sant'Angelo. Walking across connects Renaissance Rome with Vatican while appreciating Tiber River views and baroque sculpture creating one of Rome's most photogenic spots.

Aventine Hill

This quiet residential hill offers refined atmosphere, orange garden views, and the famous Priory of Malta keyhole revealing perfectly framed St. Peter's dome. Medieval churches including Santa Sabina provide tranquility. The hill represents Rome's seven hills tradition and offers escape from tourist crowds with beautiful gardens, ancient churches, and stunning panoramas. The exclusive character and hidden treasures make it perfect for discovering peaceful, elegant Rome.

Via Veneto

This elegant tree-lined boulevard became synonymous with la dolce vita in the 1960s when Fellini filmed here and celebrities frequented its cafes. The curve from Piazza Barberini to Villa Borghese features luxury hotels, embassies, and upscale cafes. Though less glamorous than its heyday, Via Veneto retains belle √©poque charm and represents Rome's mid-century golden age when it was the world's most fashionable street.

Terrazza del Pincio

This panoramic terrace at Pincio's edge provides spectacular views over Piazza del Popolo, Rome's rooftops, and St. Peter's dome. The 19th-century design includes the water clock and tree-lined promenade. Sunset attracts couples and photographers for the golden light over Rome's domes and monuments. The terrace represents romantic Rome and belle √©poque elegance while offering perfect perspective on the city's geography and architectural harmony.

Chiesa di Sant'Ignazio

This baroque church features Andrea Pozzo's extraordinary trompe-l'oeil ceiling creating illusory architecture ascending to heaven. The painted dome viewed from the marked floor spot appears three-dimensional. The theatrical baroque decoration overwhelms senses with gilt, frescoes, and marble. The church represents Jesuit Counter-Reformation art using visual splendor and optical illusion to inspire faith. Pozzo's mastery of perspective and the overwhelming baroque decoration make it unforgettable.

Ponte Umberto I

This late 19th-century bridge connects central Rome with Vatican area, featuring three arches and travertine construction. Built during Rome's post-unification expansion, the bridge represents modern engineering while respecting classical aesthetics. The bridge provides Tiber River views toward St. Peter's and Castel Sant'Angelo. Though less ornate than Ponte Sant'Angelo, its strategic location and solid neoclassical design make it functional gateway between historic center and Vatican.

Monument to Vittorio Emanuele II

This massive white marble monument nicknamed "Vittoriano" or "Wedding Cake" honors Italy's first king and unification. Completed in 1925, the neoclassical structure features the Altar of the Fatherland and Tomb of the Unknown Soldier. The rooftop terrace offers 360-degree Rome views. Though controversial architecturally, the monument represents unified Italy's aspirations and nationalism. The overwhelming scale and central location make it impossible to miss or ignore.

Piazza di Spagna

The Spanish Steps cascade down to this elegant square featuring the boat-shaped Barcaccia fountain by Bernini's father. The 135 steps connect the square with Trinit√† dei Monti church above. The piazza has been Rome's social center since the 18th century when English aristocrats stayed here during Grand Tours. Surrounded by luxury shopping and cafes, it remains Rome's most fashionable meeting point embodying romance and elegance.
