About Leipzig

Leipzig is Saxony's largest city and one of Germany's most dynamic urban centers, with a rich cultural and historical heritage. Famous as the home of Johann Sebastian Bach and as the birthplace of the 1989 Peaceful Revolution that led to the fall of the Berlin Wall, Leipzig combines musical tradition with revolutionary spirit. The city is renowned for its classical music scene, vibrant arts culture, historic trade fair tradition, and beautifully restored architecture. With its youthful energy, creative atmosphere, and historical significance, Leipzig represents both Germany's cultural depth and its modern transformation.

Leipzig Markt (Market Square)

The historic heart of Leipzig, this bustling square has been the center of commerce and civic life for centuries. Dominated by the magnificent Renaissance Old Town Hall (Altes Rathaus), now housing the City History Museum, the Markt showcases Leipzig's trading heritage. The square is surrounded by beautiful Baroque merchant houses with distinctive gabled facades. Throughout the year, the Markt hosts markets, festivals, and events, including the famous Christmas market. The square connects to Leipzig's historic shopping arcades and passages, making it the perfect starting point for exploring the city center.

Nikolaikirche (St. Nicholas Church)

This church holds monumental significance as the starting point of the 1989 Peaceful Revolution that helped bring down the Berlin Wall. Beginning with prayer services in September 1989, Monday demonstrations grew from hundreds to thousands of peaceful protesters carrying candles and chanting "We are the people." The church's interior features distinctive palm-tree columns supporting white vaulted ceilings, creating an inspiring sacred space. Founded in 1165, Nikolaikirche is Leipzig's oldest and largest church. Bach performed many cantatas here as Thomaskantor. The church remains a powerful symbol of peaceful resistance and democratic change.

Thomaskirche (St. Thomas Church)

This Gothic church is world-famous as the workplace of Johann Sebastian Bach, who served as Thomaskantor from 1723 until his death in 1750. Bach composed many of his greatest works here, and his remains are buried in the church. The renowned Thomanerchor boys' choir, founded in 1212, continues performing Bach's music in regular services and concerts. The church features beautiful stained glass windows depicting Bach and Martin Luther. A bronze statue of Bach stands outside, and the adjacent Bach Museum offers deeper insights into the composer's life. For classical music lovers, attending a service with the Thomanerchor is an unforgettable experience.

This colossal monument commemorates the 1813 Battle of Leipzig, where coalition forces defeated Napoleon's army in one of history's largest battles before the 20th century. Standing 91 meters tall, this imposing structure was completed in 1913 on the battle's centenary. The monument features massive stone sculptures representing virtues and mourning, reflecting Imperial Germany's architectural grandeur. Visitors can climb 500 steps to the viewing platform for spectacular panoramas over Leipzig and the surrounding countryside. The monument's crypt and Hall of Fame contain impressive sculptures. This powerful memorial represents a pivotal moment in European history.

Augustusplatz

One of Europe's largest city squares, Augustusplatz serves as Leipzig's cultural centerpiece. The plaza is flanked by major cultural institutions including the Opera House, the Gewandhaus concert hall (home to one of the world's oldest orchestras), and Leipzig University's modern tower. The square perfectly illustrates Leipzig's combination of classical culture and contemporary architecture. During GDR times, the square was called Karl-Marx-Platz and witnessed many political gatherings. Today, it hosts open-air concerts, markets, and festivals. The Mendebrunnen fountain adds a historic touch to this dynamic urban space.
